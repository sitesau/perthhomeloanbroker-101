Construction Loans Perth Comparison Explained

A standard home loan releases the full purchase price at settlement, meaning you start paying interest immediately on the entire balance. By contrast, construction loans perth operate on a progress payment system. The lender advances funds in stages specifically tied to your builder's progress schedule, such as the slab down, frame up, lock-up, fixing stage, and practical completion.

This staged structure is beneficial because interest is charged only on the amount that has been drawn down, not the total approved loan limit. Consequently, your repayments remain lower during the construction period compared to a full principal and interest loan from day one. This protects both the borrower and the lender, as the bank verifies that work is complete at each milestone before releasing the next tranche of funds. Evaluating Lenders on Contract Requirements

Most lenders require a fixed-price building contract before they will approve a construction loan. This contract locks in the total build cost, providing the bank with a reliable figure for its loan-to-value calculations. Without a fixed price, the lender cannot accurately assess the completed property's value or the borrower's risk exposure if costs blow out. When comparing options, it is crucial to watch for variation clauses or prime-cost items in your contract, as lenders often view these as cost unknowns.

Some lenders may require you to have contingency funds or reduce the amount they advance if the contract contains too many variables. A broker can assist by identifying which lenders handle variation clauses more flexibly. The loan-to-value ratio is generally assessed against the combined value of the land and the contract price. If this position exceeds a lender's threshold, lenders mortgage insurance may apply. Knockdown-Rebuilds and Refinancing

Knockdown-rebuilds present a different approval pathway compared to standard new builds on vacant blocks. Since you already own the land, you may have an existing mortgage over it. Your broker needs to factor in refinancing or restructuring this debt alongside the new construction finance facility. It is important to note that demolition costs are generally not included in the building contract and must be funded separately or rolled into a pre-construction budget.

For Perth suburbs where established properties sit on large blocks, a knockdown-rebuild can be a cost-effective way to secure a modern home in a preferred location. Lenders may value the security as land-only during construction before reverting to the completed dwelling value at practical completion. Brokers familiar with the Perth market can steer you toward lenders who actively support knockdown-rebuild projects rather than treating them as higher-risk exceptions.

Managing Interest-Only Repayments

During the construction phase, most lenders place the facility on interest-only repayments calculated on the drawn balance. As each progress payment is released to the builder, your monthly obligation increases incrementally. Once the builder reaches practical completion, the loan typically converts to principal-and-interest repayments on the full approved amount. The interest-only period is usually set to match the build duration outlined in the contract.

While most lenders allow for an extension if construction runs over time, this usually requires supporting documentation from the builder. A broker can model repayment scenarios across multiple lenders so you know exactly what to expect at each stage of the build. Understanding this repayment trajectory is vital for budgeting, particularly for first home buyers or those managing an existing mortgage alongside the construction loan.

Common questions

How are repayments calculated during the construction phase? Repayments are interest-only and calculated based on the amount of money that has been drawn down to date, not the total loan amount. This means your repayments increase incrementally as the builder completes each stage.

Why do lenders require a fixed-price building contract? Lenders need a fixed-price contract to reliably assess the completed property's value and the loan-to-value ratio. It reduces the risk of cost overruns that could impact the borrower's ability to repay the loan.

Can I use a construction loan for a knockdown-rebuild? Yes, but the process involves refinancing any existing mortgage on the land and separately funding demolition costs, as these are usually not covered by the building contract.
